GEOG 495 Field Trip Story Map

Here is a presentation of all of the stops we made on each of our field trips using a story map constructed using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. Each stop has an image of the location, along with a brief description of what we did or learned. In the background is a flow map of the routes we took color coded by field trip.

    This was our last field trip, which took place in the middle of May. We traveled to and from Bend, with stops in the city as well as en route to and leaving the area. We stopped at the following locations:

  • Peavy Arboretum
  • Foster Lake
  • Hackleman Old Growth Forest
  • Old Mill District
  • Oregon Observatory
  • High Desert Museum
  • Hogg Pass SNOTEL

    • Mary's Peak

    Our first stop was at Mary's Peak, the highest mountain in the coastal range. Here, we observed the type of rock that was present because of tectonic uplift, which was basalt. This gave me a more detailed understanding of the physical geography of our state and how seismic activity is fairly responsible for the terrain, mainly in the western part of the state.

    Image Link: https://wonderingaroundoregon.files.wordpress.com/2015/06/20150620_130517.jpg

    Waldport

    At this stop, we visited a small museum that displayed the history of bridges within the state of Oregon. This emphasized the importance of transporting across the state and the coast since traveling would take so much longer without bridges. A timeline was also given to track the development of bridges in the state, along with a side note of the travel time for traveling through the state.

    Image Link: https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b6/Alseabaybridge-south.jpg

    Cape Lookout

    This viewpoint along the coastal highway reveals a small bay to the right with a strip of the Beach separating the bay from the ocean. This was initially formed by part of the ridge splitting the waves off into its sides that led to deposition. These conditions allow for good grass growth that is useful for farming cattle. This allowed for the formation and growth of the Tillamook industry to take place.

    Image Link: https://i1.wp.com/www.hachettebookgroup.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/01/OR_CapeLookout_RandalKetchem123rf.jpg

    Tillamook Cheese Factory

    Because we have to support our local economy!

    Image Link: https://www.tillamook.com/image/3089.jpg

    Camp Rilea

    This is a military camp located near Astoria, which was where we spent the night. In addition to the camp, there is a small obstacle course comprised of large urban debris, which is used to prepare soldiers for navigating through and rescuing others in potential post-earthquake debris.

    Image Link: https://www.militarycampgrounds.us/media/reviews/photos/original/aa/fa/79/camp-rilea-53-1430099155.jpg

    Fort Clatsop

    This was the site of the winter fort constructed by Lewis and Clark in the early 1800s. A replica of the fort is in place for visitors to explore and a brief history of the area, information about the local Native American tribes and about the tools used for exploration are displayed. We also watched a short video of the history of Lewis and Clark's time at Fort Clatsop, which also highlighted the contrasting lifestyles of the Native Americans and expeditioners.

    Image Link: http://www.lewis-clark.org/media/new-images/fort_clatsop_048.jpg

    Columbia River Maritime Museum

    There were several different types of exhibits available within this museum. Some of them included ship design, history of ship use in the Columbia River, the danger of navigating through the river, ship use during World War II, and a display of Japanese flags from the war. The "Columbia" ship was available for a walking tour.

    Image Link: http://www.crmm.org/images/columbia-river-maritime-museum.jpg

    Vista House

    This place was an area to view the Columbia River Gorge from a high point. We discussed the effort made to protect the scenic area of the Gorge by reducing urbanization (primarily on the Washington side). This marked the end of the first field trip.

    https://isocarp.org/app/uploads/2017/08/Vista-House.jpg

    OHSU - Kohler Pavilion

    At this stop, I taught the skill of critiquing maps based on their design using knowledge presented in class about map elements and good and bad design principles. I provided two examples of what I thought were "good" maps and two that I considered to be "bad" maps, and passed them around so everyone could look at them. After everyone viewed them, I asked what made each one good or bad, what design principles were used, and how some of the maps could be improved.

    Image Link: https://perkinswill.com/sites/default/files/project-imagery/ohsu_terrace_main1.jpg

    Mount Tabor

    We learned about basemaps and their importance with respect to cartography. Specifically, what information should be included or excluded, as well as the design. Like with the previous stop, choosing a good basemap is dependent on the map's purpose.

    Image Link: https://www.ritani.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/mount-tabor.jpg

    Alberta Street

    At Alberta Street, we observed the cultural landscape of the street and the surrounding area. The street was very well upkept and modern due to gentrification. However, the surrounding neighborhoods were fairly impoverished.

    Image Link: https://splimm.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/09_alberta_mural-770x578.jpg

    Salmon Street Springs

    At this location, we observed the application of the Modifiable Areal Unit Problem, or MAUP. Specifically, we were divided intov"districts" in a way that the men, who had fewer numbers in class compared to the women, had the overallvmajority. This was essentially gerrymandering, an application of dealing with the MAUP. While solving the MAUP is used for more complex issues, the simple application of this helped solidify understanding the concept.

    Image Link: http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-qQ0kjAodyzg/UeQZXCi32UI/AAAAAAAAB5c/nbr69X2fHKI/s1600/salmon-street-springs.jpg

    Ellmaker State Park

    Two presentations took place at this spot, which involved the use of compasses. The first one was an activity that involved walking in a triangle to three different locations using a compass. The second one involved using a compass to see where you would end up compared to not using one given a set of directions for where to walk. Each ending point was expected to be different from the other.

    Image Link: https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b6/Ellmaker_State_Park.jpg

    Eddyville Roadside

    This activity took place on the side of Highway 20 near the town of Eddyville near some rocks formed by erosion. From there, a Brunton compass was used to calculate the strike and dip. This is done by measuring the angle the surface of the rock is "dipping" or inclining downward and where a perpendicular rock or surface "strikes" the inclined rock.

    Image Link: https://dmt55mxnkgbz2.cloudfront.net/900x0_s3-27649-W-PNW-040_16-LT-5_JPG.jpg

    Beverly Beach

    At this location, we used basic trigonometry to view and understand the tectonic uplift taking place along the coast. Specifically, we looked at a deposited section of fossils along a cliff on the beach and attempted to measure the height of these fossil beds with respect to sea level. This was used to observe the rise in material caused by tectonic uplift that can be used to help us better understand the future Cascadia earthquake.

    Image Link: https://www.reserveamerica.com/webphotos/originals/OR/pid402126/%7Bpht%7Dbeverly-beach-summer-shot-1%7Bpht%7D1394217474092.jpg

    Canyon Drive Park

    For the last stop of the second field trip, we stopped at Canyon Drive Park, which is located in Lincoln City. There, we saw the application of the remote sensing presentation on waves, where we learned about the science of waves. In addition, we viewed a map of the coast in the Lincon City area, which is a good place for surfing. Therefore, understanding the dynamics of the waves at this area is critical.

    Image Link: https://www.beachconnection.net/news/lc_11fog.jpg

    Peavy Arboretum

    For our first stop of the third field trip, we went to the Peavy Arboretum and learned how to examine a section of the forest in order to record all of the plant species within that section. Specifically, everyone was split into groups and each group created a section of about 3 feet by 1 foot and noted the types of plants within the section. Many of the plants were small, leafy plants, along with some ferns.

    Image Link: https://i.pinimg.com/originals/c2/0f/c1/c20fc10fd496f39a17921e97be5867ef.jpg

    Shea View Point - Foster Lake

    We learned about the application of the adaptive cycle that has taken place Foster Lake and the area around it by looking at historic imagery. The stages of this cycle are growth, conservation, release, and organization. Each of the images revealed different stages of the area, in which some notable features included presence of lumber in the town and deforestation. Knowing each of the phases ahead of time made it easier to interpret which phase of the adaptive cycle Foster Lake and the surrounding areas were in.

    Image Link: https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/25/Foster_Lake_Water_Skier.jpg

    Hackelman Old Growth Forest

    At this stop, we used a vegetation classification index to classify the types of vegetation we observed along the trail, where everyone was in different groups. The most common forms of vegetation were trees, as this was an area that was located in the mountains. This was an interesting way to gain a general understanding of the vegetation types of an area rather than simply observing it.

    Image Link: https://cdn-assets.alltrails.com/uploads/photo/image/19215105/extra_large_57b93b508b46f08cecca3fcdfda6dd19.jpg

    Old Mill District

    We learned about how the pressure and release model can be applied by observing facts about different towns, in which we looked at Bend and La Pine. Bend is a fast growing city with a majority of the population in good economic standing, which also has a couple hospitals and three fire stations. In comparison, La Pine is much smaller with a lower economic standing among the population compared to Bend, and has just one fire station. After discussing the statistics for each town, Bend does have more resources for sustainability, but La Pine may have a better network of help since it is a smaller community and therefore more connections, which can help the town's sustainability.

    Image Link: https://bt-wpstatic.freetls.fastly.net/wp-content/blogs.dir/18/files/2016/05/Old-Mill-1560x876.jpg

    Sunriver Observatory

    For our last stop of the day, we traveled to the Oregon Observatory in Sunriver. We viewed a presentation about possibly traveling to Mars, as well as viewing different stars and formations in the telescopes. Afterward, we looked at the stars in the sky and identified constellations, which are used for navigation for those at sea, which is crucial for knowing where you are headed.

    Image Link: https://visitcentraloregon.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/Oregon-Observatory_Sunriver-Oregon.jpg

    High Desert Museum

    The main stop of the final day was at the High Desert Museum. Several exhibits were on display, which included information on Native Americans, animal navigation, a replica of an old western town, and lots of animals that are native to the high desert region found in the Bend area as well as a large portion of the western U.S. While there were many remarkable features of the museum, the ones we focused on for the presentations were on animal migration and sampling. For animal migration, a section of the museum discussed different animals' ways of navigating for migration, which included having a knowledge of the magnetic field and a sense of direction, particularly north. For sampling, we discussed the basic principles of sampling, with sampling trees on the drive over as an example.

    Image Link: https://oregonencyclopedia.org/media/uploads/High_Desert_Museum.jpg

    Hogg Pass SNOTEL

    For our final stop, we took a short hike to the SNOTEL site on the road back from Bend. There, we got to see the parts that make up a SNOTEL, such as the plate that measures the depth of the snow, the machine that measures the wind, and the station that sends the data to nearby locations for analysis.

    Image Link: https://www.wcc.nrcs.usda.gov/siteimages/526.jpg

    Overall, I got a lot out of this course, in addition to the skills that were presented and taught. I have learned more about our state in a way that made me think critically about why everything is the way it is in Oregon and the difference that it makes within the realms of physical and human geography. This class also reinforced the fact that geography is a very broad field and that while we can focus on one portion of it in a deeper way, we can't say that we'll fully understand all of it and it's connections to other fields of geography and for everyday life.
